How to optimise your LinkedIn profile to win B2B clients (not just recruiters)

Most LinkedIn profile advice is written for job seekers. If you are a B2B founder, consultant, or independent leader, your profile needs to do something different: build credibility with buyers before you have spoken to them.

By Josh Huggins June 2026 8 min read

To optimise a LinkedIn profile for B2B clients rather than recruiters: rewrite your headline around the outcome you create for buyers, open your about section with the problem you solve rather than your career history, use the featured section as a landing page for your best work, and gather recommendations that name specific results rather than generic praise. Profile optimisation alone produces limited results without consistent content: the profile converts interest that content generates.

Search "LinkedIn profile optimisation" and you will find a lot of advice about how to attract recruiters. Keyword density. Skills endorsements. Getting to All-Star status.

That advice is not wrong for people looking for their next role. But if you are a B2B founder, an independent consultant, a fractional executive, or a professional services leader, your LinkedIn profile has a different job to do. It is not a CV. It is a landing page for your credibility, and it needs to be optimised for the buyers who will look you up, not the recruiters who might.

The stakes are higher than most people realise. According to the 6sense 2025 B2B Buyer Report, buyers complete 61% of their purchase decision before making first contact. That research phase involves looking up the people they are considering working with. What they find on your LinkedIn profile during that phase influences whether you end up on the shortlist before you have said a word.

The headline: the most valuable real estate on your profile

Your headline appears everywhere on LinkedIn: search results, connection requests, comments, InMails, the feed. It is the first thing a buyer reads about you and the thing that determines whether they read further.

Most professionals have a headline that reads like a job title. "Founder, Blueberry Media." "Managing Director." "Independent Consultant." These headlines tell a recruiter what you are. They tell a buyer almost nothing useful.

A headline optimised for B2B buyers names who you help and what you help them achieve. It is not a tagline or a piece of marketing copy. It is a direct statement of relevance. If the right buyer reads it and thinks "that's me," the headline has done its job.

Compare these two approaches:

Before: "Founder | Blueberry Media | LinkedIn Marketing"

After: "Helping B2B founders build LinkedIn visibility that generates inbound pipeline before the first conversation starts"

The second version tells a B2B founder with a pipeline problem exactly whether this is relevant to them. It also tells a recruiter looking for a LinkedIn marketing specialist essentially nothing, which is fine, because they are not the buyer.

The about section: open with the problem, not your career

The default about section structure is: career summary, key achievements, what you do now, call to action. This structure serves the writer's ego more than the reader's attention.

Buyers who land on your profile are asking one question in the first few seconds: is this person relevant to my situation? Your about section should answer that question before it does anything else.

Start with the problem you solve. Not "I have 20 years of experience in financial services." Start with what your clients come to you with. The situation they are in before they work with you. If you can describe their problem more precisely than they can describe it themselves, you have already demonstrated expertise before listing a single credential.

Then move to what you do and how you do it. Keep it specific. Vague language ("I help businesses grow") signals a generalist. Specific language ("I work with independent consultants to build consistent LinkedIn content that keeps them visible during the 61% of their clients' decision process that happens before any formal contact") signals someone who understands a particular problem well.

End with a clear next step. Where should an interested buyer go from here? Make the action obvious.

The featured section: your profile's second impression

Most profiles leave the featured section empty or populate it with generic company posts. This is a significant missed opportunity.

The featured section sits directly below your headline and about sections. It is visible without scrolling on desktop. A buyer who has read your headline and bio lands here next, and this is where you can give them something to do with their interest.

The most effective uses of the featured section for B2B leaders are a well-performing post that demonstrates your thinking, a case study or outcome document with a specific named result, and a direct link to your contact or booking page. Any one of these converts a profile visit into something more than a passive impression.

Experience entries: write for buyers, not HR

Standard experience entries describe responsibilities. "Responsible for managing a team of twelve." "Oversaw campaign delivery across six accounts." These read like job descriptions because they were written as job descriptions.

Buyers are not assessing whether you were qualified for your previous roles. They are trying to understand what you have actually achieved and whether that experience is relevant to their situation. Rewrite experience entries around outcomes, not activities.

"Grew LinkedIn-generated pipeline from zero to 40% of total new business revenue for a B2B SaaS client over 18 months" is more useful to a buyer than "Managed LinkedIn content strategy for B2B clients."

This matters more for independent consultants and fractional executives than for anyone else. Without a well-known employer's brand providing background credibility, every line of your profile is carrying more weight.

Recommendations: specific outcomes, not general praise

Generic recommendations are almost useless commercially. "Highly professional," "great communicator," "would definitely recommend" -- these phrases appear on every profile and carry no signal.

A recommendation that names a specific problem, describes what you did, and quantifies the outcome is worth twenty generic ones. Ask recommenders for specificity: what was the situation before you worked together, what did you do, and what changed as a result? If a recommender is willing, give them a structure to work from. The output will be more useful for both of you.

The profile is not the strategy

There is an important limit to how far profile optimisation alone takes you.

A well-optimised profile converts interest. It does not generate it. Buyers do not browse LinkedIn profiles the way they browse a directory. They land on your profile because something drew them there: a post they read, a referral from a mutual connection, a comment you left that caught their attention.

The profile is the landing page. Content is the traffic source. Getting the profile right matters, but without consistent content, the best headline in the world sits in front of very few relevant buyers.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most important part of a LinkedIn profile for winning B2B clients?

The headline, because it appears everywhere on LinkedIn: search results, connection requests, comments, messages. It is the first thing a buyer reads and the thing that determines whether they read further. A headline that names your buyer and the outcome you create is significantly more effective than a job title.

How should a consultant's LinkedIn profile differ from an employee's?

An employee's profile can rely on the employer's brand for credibility. A consultant's profile carries all the credibility work alone. That means being more specific about outcomes, more direct about who you help and what you achieve, and more intentional about the social proof you display: case study links, named outcomes, specific recommendations.

Does LinkedIn profile optimisation actually increase inbound enquiries?

Profile optimisation alone produces limited results without consistent content. The profile is what buyers find after they have already been drawn in, by a post, a referral, a mention. Its job is to confirm the impression they already have and make the next step obvious. A strong profile converts interest; content generates the interest in the first place.

Should a B2B founder have a separate LinkedIn page for their business?

A company page is worth maintaining but should not be the primary content channel. Personal profiles generate significantly more organic reach and engagement. For B2B founders specifically, the personal profile is where buyers form opinions; the company page is where they confirm the business exists and looks credible.
