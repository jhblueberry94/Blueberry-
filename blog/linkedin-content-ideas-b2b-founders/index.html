LinkedIn content ideas for B2B founders: what to post when you have nothing to say

The blank screen problem is not a creativity problem. It is a system problem. Here are the content frameworks B2B founders actually use to post consistently without spending hours writing.

By Josh Huggins June 2026 9 min read

The most reliable sources of LinkedIn content for B2B founders are the conversations they are already having: client problems they solved this week, questions they get asked repeatedly, things that turned out differently to what they expected, and observations from their sector that most people have not articulated yet. The blank screen problem is not a shortage of ideas; it is a shortage of a system for capturing and using the ideas that are already there.

Most B2B founders who are not posting consistently on LinkedIn are not holding back because they have nothing worth saying. They have plenty worth saying. What they do not have is a way to say it without it feeling like a significant undertaking every time.

The irony is that the insight that would make the most compelling LinkedIn post is usually something they said in a client meeting that morning. Or a problem they solved last week. Or the thing they have noticed happening across three different clients that no one seems to be talking about publicly.

That insight does not make it to LinkedIn because there is no system for capturing it and turning it into a post. This is the blank screen problem, and it is a systems problem, problem, not a creativity problem.

Where B2B content actually comes from

Before getting into specific ideas, it is worth being direct about the single most important principle: the content that performs commercially is specific and first-hand. It does not come from aggregating what other people have said or sharing industry news with a brief comment. It comes from what you have actually seen, done, and learned.

Generic LinkedIn posts ("5 tips for better leadership," "why communication matters," "great to be at this event") generate almost no commercial return. They might get polite engagement from your existing network. They will not put you on the shortlist of a buyer who has not met you yet.

The posts that build commercial credibility are the ones that make a relevant buyer think: this person has seen the inside of this problem. This is someone worth talking to.

With that in mind, here are the content frameworks that work reliably for B2B founders.

The observation from this week's work

This is the most sustainable source of content, and the most underused. Every week you have conversations with clients, solve problems, notice patterns, and encounter things that surprise you. Almost all of it could be a post.

The format is simple: "I noticed something this week that I have not heard articulated clearly anywhere." Then articulate it. Anonymise any client specifics. Keep the observation sharp and specific.

This works because it is genuinely original. No one else had that conversation in that context. No one else can write that post. The specificity is what signals expertise, not the claim of expertise, but the demonstration of it through observation.

A weekly habit of noting two or three things that surprised you, challenged an assumption, or confirmed something you had suspected gives you more raw material than you will ever need.

The question you keep getting asked

If you answer the same question three or more times in conversations with prospects or clients, that question is almost certainly something your broader audience is also wondering about. Write the answer as a post.

This format has a useful secondary benefit: a prospect who asks you that question in a sales conversation and later finds your post answering it in depth has just seen a demonstration of your expertise without you having to manufacture it. The post is doing pre-sales work.

The thing that most people get wrong

Counterintuitive content consistently outperforms conventional wisdom on LinkedIn. Not contrarianism for its own sake: the post needs to be true and grounded in experience, but a genuine correction of a widespread misconception in your field.

"Most people think X. In practice, Y." Structure it exactly like that. State the common assumption, then correct it based on what you have actually seen. This framing immediately positions you as someone with direct experience rather than someone repeating received wisdom.

The honest outcome post

A post that describes a real client outcome: specifically, honestly,, honestly, with enough detail to make it credible, is one of the most commercially effective things you can publish. Not a case study or a testimonial, but a brief honest account of what the situation was, what changed, and what the result was.

The key word is honest. Polished case studies read as marketing. Posts that acknowledge the complexity of the situation, or the things that did not go as planned alongside the things that did, read as genuine. Buyers respond to genuine.

Anonymise where necessary, but keep enough specificity to make it real. "A client in professional services" is fine. "A consultant" is fine. The sector-level detail is what makes the post relevant to the right reader.

The sector observation

What is changing in your sector that most people have not fully absorbed yet? What are you seeing across your client base that points to something shifting? What will look obvious in twelve months that is not obvious now?

This type of post positions you as someone with pattern recognition across many situations, which is exactly what an independent consultant, fractional executive, or senior leader should be able to offer. It is also the type of content that is hardest to replicate, because it requires genuine exposure to the sector over time.

It does not need to be groundbreaking. A precise articulation of something that is vaguely felt but not clearly stated is more valuable than a sweeping prediction about the industry's future.

The framework post

If you use a framework, process, or mental model in your work, something that helps you diagnose a problem, structure a conversation, or decide between options. That framework is a post. Explain it simply. Show how it applies. Give your audience something they can use.

These posts tend to get saved, which is now one of the strongest engagement signals on LinkedIn. When someone bookmarks a post because they want to come back to it, that is a meaningful signal that the content delivered genuine value.

What to do when you genuinely cannot think of anything

Even with good habits, there are weeks where nothing obvious presents itself. A few prompts that tend to unlock something:

What did a client say this week that made you think? What assumption did a prospect have that you had to correct in a conversation? What would you tell a junior version of yourself about your field that took you years to learn? What is the most common mistake you see made by people in your buyers' position?

Any one of these prompts, answered honestly, is a post. The answer does not need to be long. It needs to be specific and true.

The real constraint is not ideas

Most founders who are not posting consistently on LinkedIn do not have a content ideas problem. They have a production problem: turning an idea into a post takes longer than it should, and when time is tight the post does not get written.

That is the problem a content programme solves. Frequently asked questions

What should a B2B founder post on LinkedIn?

The content that generates commercial results is specific rather than generic: observations from recent client work (appropriately anonymised), counterintuitive findings from your sector, honest takes on trends affecting your buyers, and frameworks that help your audience solve a problem they already have. Generic industry news and motivational quotes generate almost no commercial return.

How often should a B2B founder post on LinkedIn?

Two to three times per week is the effective range for most B2B founders. Only 3% of LinkedIn users post more than once per week, so this frequency alone puts you in a visible minority. Consistency over time matters more than any individual post: three posts per week every week outperforms ten posts in one week followed by silence.

What LinkedIn content gets the most engagement for B2B?

In 2026, content that generates early comments from relevant people in the first 60 minutes receives the most algorithmic distribution. That tends to be content with a specific observation or counterintuitive point that prompts a reaction, rather than generic advice or news reposts. Saves are now the strongest engagement signal on LinkedIn: content that people bookmark because it is genuinely useful outperforms content that generates quick likes.

How do I come up with LinkedIn content ideas without spending hours on it?

The most reliable source of LinkedIn content is the work you are already doing: conversations with clients, problems you are currently solving, things that surprised you this week, questions you get asked repeatedly. A simple weekly habit of noting two or three observations from current work gives you more raw material than you will ever need.

Should B2B founders write long or short LinkedIn posts?

Research from The Beta Theory's 2026 LinkedIn analysis shows that conversational posts between 1,300 and 3,000 characters perform around 38% better than shorter posts. The key is not length but specificity: a 300-word post with a sharp specific observation will outperform a 1,000-word post that says nothing new. Write as much as the idea requires, no more.
