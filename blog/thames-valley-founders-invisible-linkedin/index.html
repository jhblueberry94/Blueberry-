Why Thames Valley founders are invisible on LinkedIn (and London isn't)

Thames Valley founders and consultants are largely absent from LinkedIn despite the region holding one of the UK's highest concentrations of B2B leadership outside London. The gap is cultural, not geographic — and it is closing slowly for the founders who notice it first.

By Josh Huggins · June 2026 · 9 min read

Reading and Bracknell host some of the largest enterprise technology operations in the UK. Newbury and Wokingham are home to scaling professional services firms, consultancies, and SaaS businesses with genuinely senior leadership behind them.

Search LinkedIn for founders and executives actively posting from this region and the results thin out fast.

The expertise is clearly there. The problem is that most Thames Valley leaders have quietly decided LinkedIn visibility is something other people do — usually London-based founders, agencies, or tech companies — rather than something relevant to a professional services firm in Newbury or a consultancy in Reading.

That assumption is costing pipeline. Here is why it happens, and what changes for the founders who stop making it.

We have seen this gap from the inside. Blueberry Media started in Berkshire, working with Thames Valley founders and leaders before expanding to clients across the UK. The pattern below is not theoretical — it is what we have watched play out, repeatedly, in this specific region.

The region has the leadership. It does not have the visibility.

The Thames Valley is not a minor B2B market. Reading and Bracknell are among the largest technology clusters in the UK outside London — home to UK and European headquarters for major technology and software businesses. Newbury, Wokingham, and Maidenhead carry a dense base of professional services firms, consultancies, and growth-stage companies with experienced leadership teams.

What is largely missing is consistent LinkedIn presence from the people running these businesses.

A scan of the regional B2B agency landscape confirms this from another angle: nearly every agency operating in Reading and Newbury positions around website design, SEO, and PPC for SMEs. None are built specifically around LinkedIn thought leadership or founder-led content for B2B.

The regional market has the leadership and the buyers. It does not yet have many founders helping themselves show up consistently in front of either.

This is not a market without senior, credible voices. It is a market where those voices have not yet started speaking publicly.

Why Thames Valley B2B leaders stay quiet on LinkedIn

Three patterns show up consistently in conversations with founders and executives across Berkshire and the Thames Valley.

Referral culture runs deep. Much of the Thames Valley B2B economy has grown on relationships, local networks, and repeat business. That is a real strength — until the moment a buyer with no existing connection starts searching for a provider and finds nothing.

LinkedIn reads as a London thing. Visible thought leadership tends to get associated, fairly or not, with tech founders and agency people in Shoreditch. A professional services leader in Newbury or a consultant in Wokingham often does not see themselves in that picture, even though buyers research providers in exactly the same way wherever they are based.

Nobody local is modelling it yet. Visibility builds through example. When a handful of leaders in a region start posting consistently and it visibly works, others follow. The Thames Valley has not hit that inflection point yet — which gives the early movers an unusually clear run.

The buyer does not filter by location. You are already competing with London.

This is the part that changes the calculation for LinkedIn strategy in Berkshire and the Thames Valley.

According to the 6sense 2025 B2B Buyer Report, buyers are 61% of the way through their purchasing decision before they ever contact a vendor. They are researching, reading, and quietly forming a shortlist long before any conversation starts — and a lot of that research happens on LinkedIn.

A buyer in Reading searching for a consultant, agency, or partner is not filtering results by distance. If a London-based leader has been posting consistently and a Thames Valley leader doing comparable work has not, the London name is the one the buyer has already half-decided on by the time anyone picks up the phone.

Every Thames Valley founder who is not posting on LinkedIn is doing more than staying invisible. They are quietly handing ground to competitors — often London-based — who are filling that research-phase gap in their absence.

What changes when a Thames Valley founder shows up on LinkedIn

The mechanics are no different from anywhere else. What is different is how little competition there is locally.

A founder posting consistently and credibly in a market where almost nobody else from the region is doing the same has an unusually clear run at being the visible name buyers find first. There is no crowded feed to break through. In most cases, there is no other Thames Valley voice to compete with at all.

We have seen this play out with our own Thames Valley clients. We worked with the CEO of Imabi — the safeguarding app behind Railway Guardian — for over a year. Their partnership with Transport for London contributed to a 51% increase in incident reporting on the rail network over two years.

The principle behind it is the same one we keep coming back to: there is less competition for attention in this region than almost anywhere else we work, and a correspondingly faster path to being the name buyers find during the research phase that shapes their shortlist.

The first Thames Valley founders to treat LinkedIn seriously are not walking into a crowded market. They are defining what "visible" looks like in a region where almost nobody else has tried.

Three things a Thames Valley founder can do this month

  1. Audit your current LinkedIn visibility honestly. Search your own name and your closest two or three competitors. If your last post was months ago and theirs was too, the region genuinely is wide open. If even one competitor is posting consistently, they are already ahead in the research phase.
  2. Stop treating LinkedIn as optional because "it's not really our market." The 6sense data applies to every B2B sector. If your buyers research before they contact you — and they do — your absence is a gap a competitor fills by default.
  3. Start with consistency over polish. A founder posting twice a week with real opinions and lived experience will outperform a quarterly, over-produced piece every time. The region does not need perfect. It needs present.

How this connects to the wider B2B buyer journey

This regional gap is a local instance of a pattern that holds everywhere in B2B. Buyers complete most of their decision-making before they make contact. The founders who show up during that research phase — regardless of where they are based — are the ones who end up on the shortlist.

The ones who do not show up are not in the conversation at all.

Frequently asked questions

Why don't more Thames Valley founders use LinkedIn?

Much of the region's B2B economy has grown on referrals and existing relationships, which reduces the perceived need for public visibility. Many leaders also associate consistent LinkedIn posting with London-based tech and agency culture rather than something relevant to their own business — even though the same buyer research behaviour applies regardless of location.

Does LinkedIn visibility matter if my business is based outside London?

Yes. Buyers researching providers on LinkedIn do not filter results by location. A consistent, credible voice based in Reading, Newbury, or anywhere else in the Thames Valley competes directly with London-based competitors during the buyer's research phase — which happens well before first contact.

Is the Thames Valley B2B market big enough to justify LinkedIn content?

Yes. Reading and Bracknell are among the largest technology clusters in the UK outside London, and Newbury, Wokingham, and Maidenhead host a dense base of professional services and growth-stage businesses. The leadership and buyer base are both substantial. Visibility is what is currently missing.

What is the fastest way to start building LinkedIn visibility in this region?

Consistency beats polish. Two to three posts a week built around real opinions and lived professional experience, sustained over months, will outperform infrequent, highly-produced content. The goal is to be present during the buyer's research phase, not to win an awards panel.

Has Blueberry Media actually worked with Thames Valley founders?

Yes. Blueberry Media started in Berkshire. Our earliest client relationships were built with founders and leaders across the Thames Valley before the business expanded to work with clients across the UK remotely. This is not a region we are targeting from the outside.
